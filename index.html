What is Knee OA

A Progressive Joint
Degenerative Disease

Knee Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common form of arthritis, affecting over 250 million people worldwide. It is characterized by the gradual breakdown of articular cartilage — the protective tissue covering the ends of bones in the knee joint.

As cartilage wears away, bones begin to rub against each other causing pain, stiffness, and loss of function. The disease is progressive and irreversible, making early detection critical.

Primary Causes

Age, obesity, previous joint injury, repetitive stress from occupations or sports, genetic predisposition, and metabolic conditions like diabetes.

Early Warning Signs

Morning stiffness lasting under 30 minutes, aching during or after activity, swelling around the knee, and a grinding or crunching sensation with movement.

Disease Progression

OA progresses through 5 Kellgren-Lawrence grades (0–4). Joint space narrows, osteophytes (bone spurs) form, and subchondral bone thickens over years.

Who Is at Risk

Women over 55, people with BMI >30, athletes in high-impact sports, and individuals with prior knee injuries or family history of OA are most susceptible.

Kellgren–Lawrence Scale

5 Stages of Knee OA Progression

Traditional grading assigns discrete grades 0–4. Our AI outputs a continuous score within each grade, enabling detection of subtle progression.

0
Normal
No radiographic features of osteoarthritis. Healthy joint space and smooth bone surfaces.
1
Doubtful
Possible marginal osteophyte. Joint space normal. Clinically insignificant changes.
2
Mild
Definite osteophytes. Possible narrowing of joint space. Structural changes visible on X-ray.
3
Moderate
Multiple osteophytes, definite joint space narrowing, some sclerosis and possible deformity.
4
Severe
Large osteophytes, marked narrowing, severe sclerosis, definite bone deformity.
Our AI Framework

Three-Stage Learning Pipeline

Inspired by anomaly detection and few-label learning — our system learns what healthy knees look like, then measures how far a patient's X-ray deviates from that baseline.

SS-FewSOME

Self-Supervised Few-Shot Anomaly Detection learns robust healthy knee representations using contrastive learning — requiring less than 3% labelled data. Creates a compact "normal" latent space.

Stage 1 — Feature Learning

CLIP Denoising

Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training validates pseudo-labels assigned to unlabelled scans. Filters imaging artifacts (metal implants, poor quality) ensuring only clean samples enter training.

Stage 2 — Label Refinement

DCRL Scoring

Dual Centre Representation Learning constructs two feature spaces — normal and abnormal. The Euclidean distance between centres maps directly to a precise continuous OA severity score 0–4.

Stage 3 — Continuous Grading

Treatment Options

Managing Knee OA
Across All Grades

Treatment is tailored to severity. Early intervention significantly slows progression and improves quality of life. Most patients manage OA effectively without surgery.

Lifestyle & Exercise

First-line management for all grades of OA focuses on reducing load and building muscle support.

  • Low-impact aerobics: swimming, cycling
  • Quadriceps strengthening exercises
  • Weight loss — every 1 kg reduces knee load by 4 kg
  • Avoid high-impact activities during flare-ups

Medical Treatment

Pharmacological options are added progressively as OA severity increases.

  • Paracetamol / NSAIDs (Grade 1–2)
  • Topical anti-inflammatories (diclofenac gel)
  • Corticosteroid injections (Grade 3)
  • Hyaluronic acid (viscosupplementation)
  • Duloxetine for chronic pain

Physiotherapy

Structured physio is effective across all grades and delays surgical intervention.

  • Manual therapy and mobilisation
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical stimulation)
  • Hydrotherapy / aquatic rehabilitation
  • Gait retraining and orthotics

Supportive Aids

Assistive devices reduce joint stress and improve daily function significantly.

  • Knee braces (unloader or support type)
  • Orthopedic insoles and footwear
  • Walking aids (cane on opposite side)
  • RICE protocol for acute flares

Injections & Biologics

For moderate-to-severe OA that does not respond to conservative care.

  • Corticosteroid injections (short-term relief)
  • PRP (platelet-rich plasma) — emerging evidence
  • Stem cell therapy (investigational)
  • Botulinum toxin for pain management

Surgical Options

Reserved for severe OA (Grade 4) unresponsive to all other treatments.

  • Arthroscopy (limited evidence)
  • High tibial osteotomy (younger patients)
  • Unicompartmental knee replacement
  • Total knee replacement (TKR) — gold standard for Grade 4
Self-Management

What You Can Do at Home

Daily habits have a profound impact on OA progression. Simple lifestyle modifications can reduce pain and slow cartilage loss over years.

Daily Exercise Routine

Gentle quad sets, straight leg raises, and heel slides for 20–30 min. Avoid high-impact exercises. Warm up before, cool down after.

Anti-Inflammatory Diet

Increase omega-3 (fish, flaxseed), turmeric, and antioxidants. Reduce processed sugar, refined carbs, and red meat to decrease systemic inflammation.

Weight Management

Even 5% body weight reduction reduces knee pain significantly. Every kilogram lost removes 4 kg of force from the knee joint during walking.

Heat & Cold Therapy

Apply cold packs for acute swelling (20 min, 3x/day). Use warm compress before exercise to loosen stiff joints. Never apply directly to skin.

Sleep & Rest

Use a pillow between knees when sleeping on your side. Avoid prolonged sitting or standing. Take regular movement breaks every 45 minutes.

Hydration

Cartilage is 80% water. Drinking 2–3 litres daily helps maintain joint lubrication and cartilage elasticity. Avoid dehydration especially in warm climates.

Vitamin D & Calcium

Low Vitamin D accelerates OA progression. Supplement 800–1000 IU Vitamin D3 daily. Include calcium-rich foods: dairy, leafy greens, almonds.

Mental Wellbeing

Chronic pain affects mental health. Mindfulness, gentle yoga, and pacing activities prevent the pain cycle. Join OA support groups for shared management strategies.

Medical Attention

When to See
a Doctor

Morning stiffness > 30 minutes

Prolonged stiffness after waking suggests active joint inflammation that needs clinical assessment.

Pain worsening over weeks

Gradual increase in pain or new areas of discomfort may indicate rapid OA progression needing imaging.

Difficulty with daily activities

Trouble climbing stairs, rising from a chair, or walking >500m indicates functional decline requiring assessment.

Joint swelling or warmth

Sudden swelling, redness, or warmth may indicate effusion or secondary infection requiring urgent evaluation.

Locking or giving way of the knee

Mechanical symptoms like the knee locking or buckling suggest intra-articular pathology needing urgent orthopaedic review.

Seek Immediate Care if…

These symptoms require emergency orthopaedic evaluation and should not be managed at home or delayed.

  • Sudden severe pain after a fall or trauma
  • Complete inability to bear weight on the knee
  • Visible deformity of the knee joint
  • Fever with joint pain (septic arthritis)
  • Rapidly increasing swelling within hours
  • Numbness or tingling down the leg
